Yes. I watched through the presentation visually frame-by-frame. The biggest thing I see is this:

The tool looks powerful, but the sales flow still feels more like a software/report walkthrough than a psychologically guided buying journey.

That is fixable. The structure is close. You already have the right ingredients: score, contaminants, family impact, home impact, live test, system recommendation, proof, savings, pricing, QR handoff. But the emotional pacing and trust sequencing need work.

My Main Diagnosis

Right now the presentation does this:

Collect data → show water score → show contaminants → show impacts → show system → show proof → show investment → show QR

That is logical.

But a buyer does not emotionally process it that way.

A better high-ticket in-home sales flow should feel like:

“I trust this person” → “This is about my home” → “I didn’t know that” → “That concerns me” → “This explains things I’ve noticed” → “There is a clear fix” → “This company seems credible” → “The price makes sense compared to the problem” → “I feel safe saying yes.”

Your app currently has the information, but it needs more emotional choreography.

What You Should Change First
1. The opening needs a stronger “human agenda” before the score

The first real screen says “Welcome Janet” and then jumps into “Let’s See Your Water Score.”

That is clean, but it is too quick. The consumer has not yet been emotionally prepared for what the score means.

Before showing the score, add a short screen that frames the presentation.

Something like:

“Today we’re going to look at three things:”

What is in the water serving your home
How it may affect your family, home, and appliances
What can be done to improve it if you choose to

Then add:

“This is not a pass/fail test. It is a clarity report so you can make an informed decision.”

Why this matters:
If you show a scary score too early, skeptical buyers may feel like they are being manipulated. If you explain the rules first, the score feels more credible.

2. The water score is good, but the scoring system needs context

The score screen showed a score around 30/100 and labeled the water as At Risk.

That is attention-grabbing, but it creates a trust problem if the buyer does not understand how the score was calculated.

You need a “score explanation” moment before or directly under the score.

Add a simple breakdown:

Your Water Score is based on:

contaminants detected
levels above health guidelines
hardness/mineral levels
disinfectant levels
whole-home usage exposure
live in-home test results

Then show:

90–100 Excellent
70–89 Good
50–69 Needs Improvement
0–49 Needs Attention

I would be careful with the label “At Risk.” It is powerful, but it may feel fear-based. I would use:

Needs Attention

or

High Concern

“Your water is at risk” can sound like a scare tactic.
“Your water needs attention” sounds more professional and consultative.

3. The contaminant list is too dense too early

The contaminant screen shows 23 contaminants detected, with categories like illegal, health, detected, etc.

That is impressive, but it is visually heavy.

The average consumer is not going to process a long contaminant table. They will either skim it, get overwhelmed, or mentally disengage.

You should keep the full list available, but lead with a simplified “Top Concerns” section.

Instead of immediately showing the full contaminant list, show:

Your Top 3 Water Concerns
1. Disinfection Byproducts

Commonly linked to chlorine treatment and long-term exposure concerns.

2. Hardness / Minerals

Can affect appliances, fixtures, skin, laundry, and plumbing.

3. Chlorine / Taste / Odor

Can affect drinking water, showers, cooking, and daily comfort.

Then add a button:

View Full Contaminant Breakdown

Why this works:
Consumers buy from clarity. Too much technical proof too early creates cognitive overload.

4. Make the report feel more “local” and less generic

The report says Myrtle Beach City or similar utility data. That is good, but it should feel even more personal.

Right now it feels like:

“Here is a water report.”

It should feel like:

“This is the water profile connected to your home.”

Change wording to:

“This report is based on the utility serving your address.”

Add:

“This is not a national average.”

And if possible:

“Homes in this service area commonly show these same water concerns.”

That one line creates authority and local relevance.

5. The family impact section is one of the strongest parts — keep it, but simplify it

The “How Your Water Affects Your Family” section is good. It connects water to skin, hair, drinking, babies, etc.

But the copy needs to be more conversational and less like a health warning.

For example, instead of:

“Every shower is stripping your skin.”

Use:

“Chlorine and hard minerals can leave skin feeling dry after showers.”

That sounds more believable.

The strongest framing is:

“This is not just drinking water. This is the water your family uses every day for showers, cooking, coffee, ice, laundry, dishes, and bathing.”

That sentence should be repeated somewhere in the presentation because it reframes the whole product from a “water filter” to a whole-home protection system.

First mention it briefly during problem education.

Then bring it back right before pricing as the financial justification.

The buyer needs to hear:

“This is not only a health/comfort issue. It can also be a home maintenance and appliance issue.”

That gives them logical permission to spend money.

7. The live water test should happen earlier

The live test appears around the middle, after the contaminant and impact sections.

I would move the live test earlier.

Best sequence:

Because the live test creates belief.

Public data is credible, but physical demonstration is emotionally stronger.

A homeowner may think:

But when they see their own tap water tested, it becomes harder to dismiss.

The live test should be the “oh wow” moment.

11. The pricing screen is the biggest problem

This is the section I would fix immediately.

In the video, the investment screen showed things like:

Project price: $0
Exclusive price: $0
Then discounts created a negative number like -$840
Then financing showed a negative monthly number.

That cannot happen in front of a customer.

Even if it is because this was a demo, the app should never allow that state.

This destroys trust instantly.

You need safeguards:

hide investment screen until a valid system price is entered
do not allow discounts to exceed total price
do not show financing if price is $0
do not show negative monthly payments
do not show “save $0”
require dealer to enter price before moving forward
show “Price pending” only on dealer side, never consumer side

This is not a small bug. This is a sales-killer.

A buyer seeing a broken price screen subconsciously thinks:

“This company is not professional.”
