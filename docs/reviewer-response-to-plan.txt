Your phase plan is strong.

Phase 1 — Foundation Fixes

I agree with starting here because these are trust-critical.

Pricing guardrails are the top priority. The customer-facing pricing screen should never show $0, negative discounts, negative final price, negative savings, or negative monthly payments. That is the kind of bug that instantly kills credibility in the home.

The score methodology explainer is also important because the score is emotionally powerful, but without context it can feel arbitrary or manipulative. A simple explainer gives it legitimacy.

The Top 3 Concerns component is exactly right. Lead with simple human-readable concerns, then let the full contaminant table be expandable. This keeps the proof without overwhelming the homeowner.

The Final Summary screen is also important because the current QR handoff feels functional but not emotionally complete. The homeowner needs a conclusion before the report delivery.

Phase 2 — Flow Rebuild

This is where the product really starts becoming a sales weapon.

The new DemoHomeProfile component is important because it makes the report feel personal before the score appears. The homeowner needs to feel, “This is about my house,” before they see a 30/100 or any concerning result.

The DemoCustomerConcerns screen is worth building now. I would not backlog it. Even a simple version adds a lot of value.

“What matters most to you?” does more than collect data. It creates a micro-commitment. If the homeowner selects family, drinking water, skin/hair, appliances, taste, odor, stains, or bottled water costs, they are telling the system and the rep what emotional angle matters most. Even if the only first version is reordering or emphasizing sections based on that answer, it will make the presentation feel much more custom.

Moving DemoLiveTest earlier is one of the biggest conversion improvements. The ideal sequence is:

Local data says this.
Now let’s verify your tap water.
The live test confirms it.

That is much stronger than showing a wall of data first and making the homeowner wait for the physical proof.

Moving DemoExpenses before DemoPricing is also right. Cost-of-inaction should anchor the price before the investment is shown. The buyer should feel like they are already paying for poor water through bottled water, filters, appliance wear, plumbing buildup, cleaning products, and comfort issues. Then the system price becomes a comparison instead of a random large number.

The DemoDecisionPage is a very strong addition. Ending with only QR/report delivery feels unfinished. The final decision page should give the rep a clean structured close:

- Move forward today
- Schedule a follow-up
- Send my report

That way even a “not today” still becomes a captured next step instead of a dead end.

Phase 3 — Emotional Polish

This is where the wizard starts feeling elite.

The DemoScoreJourney component is one of the most important screens in the whole product. The score transformation should be treated like a major emotional moment, not just a comparison widget.

The visual should clearly show:

Starting Score → Verified Score → Projected Score

Example:
30 → 55 → 94

That is the “wow” moment. It gives the homeowner a before/after transformation they can immediately understand.

I also agree with merging DemoFamilyImpact and DemoHomeImpact into one section, but not flattening them too much. They are two different buying triggers.

Family Impact = heart / protection / emotion
Home Impact = wallet / maintenance / financial logic

The ideal layout is probably one section with two big cards:

Your Family
- drinking
- cooking
- showers
- skin
- hair
- kids
- pets

Your Home
- appliances
- plumbing
- water heater
- fixtures
- laundry
- scale buildup

That lets both emotional triggers stay distinct while keeping the flow tight.

The system recommendation should definitely be benefits-first, technical second. Homeowners care first about what changes in daily life, not the internal parts of the system.

Lead with:
- cleaner water throughout the home
- better drinking and cooking water
- improved shower comfort
- reduced chlorine taste and odor
- appliance and plumbing protection
- less hard water buildup

Then make the technical specs expandable:
- carbon filtration
- sediment filtration
- softener
- smart valve
- brine tank
- bypass valve
- installation details

DemoBoost should be renamed to Optional Premium Protection. “Boost” can make the base recommendation feel incomplete, like they are being upsold after being told the system solves the problem. Optional Premium Protection feels more professional.

Phase 4 — Smart Personalization

This is where the product becomes hard to compete with.

The concern selection should eventually feed into presentation emphasis and ordering.

Examples:

If the homeowner selects Family Health:
- show Family Impact before Home Impact
- emphasize drinking, cooking, showers, kids, pets
- use protective language

If they select Appliances / Plumbing:
- show Home Impact first
- emphasize hard water, scale, water heater, dishwasher, laundry, fixtures
- lean into cost-of-inaction

If they select Taste / Odor:
- emphasize chlorine, drinking water, cooking, ice, coffee
- show taste/odor sections earlier

If they select Skeptical / Just Want Data, or if the rep flags them as skeptical later:
- emphasize methodology
- expand contaminants
- show proof, certifications, reviews, source data earlier

This does not need to be full AI at first. A lightweight context/state layer is enough.

One additional recommendation: consider eventually adding a Presentation State Engine.

This would track things like:
- selected primary concern
- household size
- kids/pets
- bottled water usage
- current filter setup
- homeowner concern type
- spouse present or not
- urgency level
- skepticism level
- objection markers

Then the wizard can intelligently emphasize sections without needing full AI.

Example:

If concern = family and kids = yes:
- emphasize family impact
- soften copy into protective language
- show daily-use examples

If concern = appliances:
- emphasize home impact and cost-of-inaction
- show water heater, dishwasher, plumbing, laundry, fixtures

If skeptical = true:
- show methodology, proof, reviews, certifications, and source data more prominently

This would turn the wizard from a static report into an adaptive sales presentation engine.

Another big opportunity: transitional narration.

The screens matter, but the transitions between screens are what will make this feel like a premium guided presentation instead of software.

The rep needs short bridge language, not full scripts.

Examples:

Before Live Test:
“The report gives us the local water picture. Now let’s verify what is actually coming out of your tap today.”

Before Family/Home Impact:
“Now that we’ve verified the water profile, the next question is what this means inside the home every day.”

Before Expenses:
“Most homeowners do not realize they may already be paying for poor water in other ways.”

Before Pricing:
“Now that we’ve looked at the problem, the impact, and the solution, let’s look at what it would take to fix it.”

These could be dealer-only cues/tooltips. Not visible to the customer. Not a full script. Just emotional bridge prompts.

This would help average reps present more like top reps.
